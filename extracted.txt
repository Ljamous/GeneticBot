Abstract:
Genetic counselling is essential for hereditary-cancer care, but access remains limited because pre-test risk assessment is time-intensive and specialist dependent. We developed GeneticBot, a web-based decision aid intended to support pre-test counselling and structured eligibility assessment for hereditary breast cancer testing. The prototype integrates guided data entry, pedigree capture, retrieval of relevant NCCN passages, and generation of rationale-rich summaries for patients and clinicians. In this phase-1 study, we evaluated feasibility and decision-level concordance using 50 fully synthetic, non-identifiable cases created to span common, borderline, and rare testing scenarios. Each case was reviewed using a traditional reference assessment based on the same guideline framework and was processed twice by the system to assess reproducibility. GeneticBot matched the reference eligibility decision in 49/50 cases (98%), with sensitivity 1.00, specificity 0.91, and Cohen’s κ 0.94; exact narrative agreement was observed in 46/50 cases (92%). The single discordant case reflected misapplication of one criterion, whereas the remaining narrative differences did not alter the binary eligibility decision. These findings support the feasibility of the prototype and its ability to reproduce guideline-based eligibility decisions within synthetic scenarios. They do not establish clinical validity, patient benefit, or deployment readiness. Prospective real-world evaluation is needed to assess usability, safety, workflow impact, and generalizability.
Author Summary
Access to pre-test genetic counselling for hereditary breast cancer is limited in many settings because the process is time-intensive and requires specialized expertise. We developed GeneticBot, a web-based prototype designed to support, not replace, counsellor-led care. The system guides users through structured data entry, pedigree capture, and NCCN-aligned eligibility prompts, and it produces rationale-rich summaries that may support documentation and triage. To test early feasibility, we created 50 fully synthetic cases rather than using real patient data. Each case was reviewed manually against the same guideline framework and processed twice by the prototype. GeneticBot matched the reference eligibility decision in 49 of 50 cases, while exact narrative agreement was seen in 46 cases. These findings show that the prototype can reproduce guideline-based eligibility decisions in controlled synthetic scenarios and that its binary outputs are reproducible. However, this was not a clinical validation study, and we did not assess patient outcomes, usability, safety in practice, or performance with real-world data. Further prospective evaluation is needed before any clinical deployment.
1. Introduction
Genetic counselling is central to identifying individuals at elevated risk of hereditary cancers, particularly breast cancer. Guideline-based pre-test assessment requires careful review of personal history, tumour features, and multigenerational family history, which can be time-intensive and difficult to scale when specialist availability is limited. Structured digital tools may therefore help standardize intake, improve documentation, and support consistent pre-test triage without replacing clinical judgement (1,2).
Approximately 5–10% of breast cancers are attributable to inherited pathogenic variants, most commonly in BRCA1 and BRCA2 (3). In population-based cohorts, pathogenic variants across established breast-cancer predisposition genes are detected in about 5% of unselected cases (4). At the same time, multigene panel testing and massively parallel sequencing have increased the complexity of pre-test counselling by expanding the number of genes considered and the frequency of uncertain findings, including variants of uncertain significance (8–12). Service innovations such as telegenetics, mainstreaming, decision aids, and structured digital intake tools have therefore received increasing attention as ways to improve access and efficiency while preserving informed decision-making (6,7,17–19).
Across the Middle East, genomic medicine is increasingly prioritized, with regional initiatives, training programs, and service expansion underway in several countries (5,13–16). Despite this progress, access to specialized genetic counselling remains uneven, and services often depend on a limited workforce, physician-led pathways, or ad hoc referral processes. These constraints are particularly relevant for hereditary-cancer assessment, where guideline application requires detailed and accurate family-history collection, pathology interpretation, and clear communication of testing benefits, limitations, and uncertainties.
Against this background, we developed GeneticBot, a web-based decision aid intended to support pre-test counselling for high-risk breast cancer. The aim of the present study was not to clinically validate the tool, but to describe its design and to perform an early-stage feasibility and concordance evaluation using fully synthetic cases. Specifically, we assessed whether the prototype could reproduce guideline-based eligibility decisions under controlled synthetic scenarios, while also documenting its workflow, safeguards, and current limitations.
These challenges underscore the need for digital approaches that simplify pre-test education, bring consistency to risk assessment, and cut administrative load. Telemedicine, decision-support systems, and interactive tools can structure education, standardize evaluations, and lessen the workload for genetic counsellors . To address this gap, our study developed GeneticBot, a web-based decision aid that operationalizes the NCCN Guidelines to automate pre-test eligibility assessment and provide structured, accessible pre-test genetic counselling. By embedding National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) criteria within a digital decision-support framework, the system aims to enhance accessibility, standardize triage, and support informed decision-making in regions where trained genetic counsellors are limited.
2. Methods
2.1 Ethical compliance
Use of NCCN content for this project was covered by formal permission from the National Comprehensive Cancer Network. The present manuscript reports prototype development and a synthetic-case evaluation only.
The validation dataset consisted exclusively of fully synthetic, non-identifiable scenarios constructed for this study. No real patient data, patient interaction, or participant-reported outcomes were collected, and healthcare professionals were not enrolled as research participants. Accordingly, the phase reported here did not involve human-subject data collection. The Likert-scale and user-evaluation instruments included in Supporting Data 7 were prepared for a future phase-2 study and were not administered in the present work. Any future real-world evaluation involving patients or clinicians will require prospective ethics review before implementation. However IRB exemption or non-human-subjects determination approval are obtained form KHCC.
2.2 Design Science Research Methodology (DSRM)
This study used Design Science Research Methodology (DSRM) to guide iterative development of a web-based decision aid for pre-test genetic counselling in high-risk breast cancer (20–22). DSRM was selected because the objective was to design, refine, and preliminarily evaluate a practical digital artifact rather than to test a causal clinical hypothesis. Within this framework, development proceeded through problem definition, objective setting, prototype design, demonstration, and communication of findings.
The DSRM process was used to identify core workflow needs, translate guideline requirements into structured decision logic, and refine the prototype through staged review. The framework also helped separate what was evaluated in the current manuscript—namely prototype design and synthetic-case concordance—from what remains for future work, including usability testing, prospective workflow studies, and patient-centered evaluation.

2.2.2 Design, development, demonstration, and evaluation
Design and development proceeded iteratively. The first increment identified limitations in conventional pre-test counselling workflows, including variable documentation, time-intensive family-history collection, and limited access to specialist counselling. The second increment translated relevant NCCN testing criteria into a structured knowledge base and decision flow. The third increment implemented these elements in a web application that supports account creation, structured intake, pathology and pedigree capture, educational content delivery, and generation of printable summaries. The front end was built using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript; the back end used PHP, MySQL, and OpenAI-supported components for retrieval and narrative generation.
The prototype combines features that are often distributed across separate tools—guided intake, pedigree capture or upload, guideline-aligned eligibility prompts, and user-facing explanatory summaries—within a single workflow. We do not claim that each component is individually unprecedented. Rather, the contribution of the current study is the integration and early evaluation of these components in a web-based prototype tailored to the stated use case.
Demonstration and evaluation focused on expert review of the framework and synthetic-case testing of the eligibility workflow. The literature-informed design phase was used to identify care gaps and define required functions; it was not intended to demonstrate patient benefit. The current manuscript reports only the synthetic-case concordance study. Human-in-the-loop review in this phase occurred during synthetic-case construction, traditional-reference adjudication, and post hoc review of generated reports. Planned future evaluations, but not yet performed here, include questionnaires on patient understanding, comparative workflow assessment, and real-world implementation testing.
2.2.3 Communication and future work
Findings from the present phase are intended to inform future journal reporting, conference dissemination, and subsequent prospective evaluation. Future work will focus on usability, human oversight in practice, and real-world validation with appropriate ethics review.
2.3 Synthetic-case construction and reference standard
To evaluate early feasibility and concordance with the guideline framework, a genetic counsellor and an oncologist constructed 50 fully synthetic cases (see Supporting Data 2 and 3). Each case included a three-generation pedigree, a pathology report, and a structured spreadsheet capturing the variables needed for both the traditional reference assessment (TA) and the decision-aid system (DAS). The TA eligibility decision was adjudicated against NCCN Version 3.2024 criteria before comparison with the prototype outputs.
The case mix was intentionally designed to span common, borderline, and rare testing scenarios so that the prototype could be stress-tested across a broad range of rule combinations. This strategy was appropriate for phase-1 feasibility work, but it does not represent a prevalence-weighted clinical sample and should not be interpreted as a real-world performance cohort.
All pedigrees and pathology reports were entered into the application by two users who were not trained in cancer genetics, and operators were blinded to the traditional reference labels during system runs. The system was executed twice for each case, generating two independent machine-produced reports. A genetic counsellor subsequently reviewed the outputs for completeness, consistency with the reference assessment, and clarity of the cited eligibility triggers (see Supporting Data 4 and 5).
2.4 System architecture, retrieval framework, and safeguards
We implemented a retrieval-augmented framework to operationalize NCCN Version 3.2024 for pre-test eligibility assessment and counselling summaries. The system indexed a structured guideline knowledge base for semantic retrieval. For each case, the application captured key clinical and pedigree variables, retrieved the most relevant guideline passages, and assembled a grounded prompt for GPT-4 to generate a rationale-rich report. In the evaluated prototype, eligibility remained anchored to explicit NCCN triggers identified from the structured case inputs; the language model was used to organize explanatory text rather than to introduce new eligibility rules. Safeguards included retrieval restricted to the authorized guideline corpus, explicit reporting of cited triggers, duplicate system runs, operator blinding, and post hoc expert adjudication. The sample size of 50 synthetic cases was selected pragmatically for phase-1 feasibility and rule-coverage testing, not for formal equivalence or non-inferiority analysis (23).
Human-in-the-loop oversight was defined at three points: (1) intake, where the operator confirms extracted pedigree and pathology fields before analysis; (2) pre-output, where the generated eligibility call and cited NCCN triggers are reviewed before being used in counselling; and (3) post-output, where clinician corrections are logged to inform prompt or rule refinement. GeneticBot is not intended to issue an autonomous clinical decision.
When family history or other NCCN-relevant fields are incomplete, the prototype is intended to flag the case as "insufficient information" and route it for clinician review rather than default to an ineligible label. Outputs that do not map cleanly to explicit guideline triggers are intended to be withheld until reviewed.
2.5 Statistical analysis
We summarized the synthetic cohort using ranges, means, and frequency distributions for age, receptor status, and pedigree complexity. The same dataset was independently processed twice, in random order, by DAS operators blinded to the traditional reference assessment. For each run, we cross-tabulated DAS output against the reference label to obtain confusion matrices and calculated sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, negative predictive value, overall accuracy, balanced accuracy, and exact 95% confidence intervals. Chance-corrected agreement was summarized with Cohen’s κ, and the exact McNemar test was used descriptively to assess whether the rare discordant calls were directionally asymmetric. Because this framework was specifically requested in peer review, we also performed an exploratory non-inferiority sensitivity analysis using a Two One-Sided Tests (TOST) framework on the overall concordance proportion with a prespecified margin of δ = 5%; this analysis was not met and is reported only to show that formal equivalence claims are not supported by the present sample. Because the evaluated output was binary and largely rule-constrained, these analyses are reported as concordance measures for an early feasibility study rather than as evidence of clinical equivalence or deployment readiness. All analyses were performed in R 4.4.1.
3. Results
3.1 Development outputs from the problem-definition phase
The problem-definition phase identified several practical barriers in pre-test genetic counselling workflows, including limited specialist availability, time-intensive family-history collection, variable documentation, and the challenge of delivering consistent pre-test education across settings. These observations, derived from the literature and workflow review, informed the functional requirements for the prototype.
This phase did not evaluate clinical outcomes. Instead, it was used to define the design goals of the prototype: structured intake, pedigree capture, guideline-aligned eligibility assessment, clear patient-facing education, auditable rationale generation, and support for human-supervised triage.
3.2 Prototype workflow and knowledge-base framework
The prototype comprises two linked components: a decision-aid system and a Chatbot. The decision-aid workflow begins with introductory educational content, then guides users through structured entry of personal and clinical information and three-generation pedigree capture. These inputs are organized for eligibility assessment against NCCN-based criteria and for generation of rationale-rich summaries intended to support pre-test counselling and documentation.
The Chatbot was designed as a supportive information layer rather than an autonomous decision-maker. Its purpose is to answer frequently asked questions, reinforce educational content, and explain common topics relevant to hereditary breast cancer counselling, including inheritance, multigene testing, potential results, and selected high-penetrance genes. Questionnaires on patient understanding and comparative workflow evaluation were developed as future study instruments, but they were not empirically tested in the present manuscript.
3.3 Synthetic-case concordance analysis
3.3.1 Validation cohort
We evaluated 50 fully synthetic breast cancer cases expressly built for this study. Each case comprised a three-generation pedigree, pathology report, and a structured spreadsheet capturing every variable needed for the NCCN Version 3.2024 review and the DAS comparison. The cohort spans women aged 29–76 years (mean 51.8 years), with cancers first diagnosed between 28 and 75 years (mean 49.6 years). Biologically, tumors were evenly split by estrogen-receptor status (26 ER-positive and 24 ER-negative), tended toward progesterone-receptor negativity (19 PR-positive and 31 PR-negative), and showed a slight majority of HER2-negative disease (21 HER2-positive and 29 HER2-negative). Pedigree complexity ranged from simple to complex across the validation set. These characteristics describe the synthetic test set used for concordance analysis; they should not be interpreted as representing a clinical prevalence sample (see Table 1).

3.3.2 Decision-level concordance and narrative exactness
The primary performance measure was decision-level concordance with the traditional reference assessment. GeneticBot matched the binary eligibility decision in 49 of 50 synthetic cases (98%). Separately, exact narrative agreement with the traditional assessment was observed in 46 of 50 cases (92%). Three additional cases had minor narrative discrepancies that did not change the eligibility classification, whereas one case represented a true criterion misapplication.
In confusion-matrix terms, the comparison with the reference assessment comprised 39 true positives, 10 true negatives, 1 false positive, and 0 false negatives, corresponding to sensitivity 1.00 (95% CI 0.91–1.00), specificity 0.91 (95% CI 0.63–0.98), positive predictive value 0.98 (95% CI 0.87–1.00), negative predictive value 1.00 (95% CI 0.72–1.00), overall accuracy 0.98, balanced accuracy 0.955, and Cohen’s κ 0.94 (see Tables 2 and 3). These values indicate high agreement within the synthetic test environment, but they should not be interpreted as clinical validation because both the cases and the reference standard were constructed within the same guideline framework.

3.3.3 Reproducibility of decision outputs
Internal reproducibility of the binary eligibility output was perfect: the two independent DAS runs agreed on all 50 cases (40 positive and 10 negative calls; κ = 1.0). This indicates that the prototype generated stable decision labels when the same synthetic inputs were processed twice. Because the evaluated workflow relied on structured inputs and rule-constrained logic, this result reflects internal consistency rather than robustness across real-world variability.
3.3.4 Agreement with the traditional reference assessment
Across the 50-case validation set, the DAS reached the same binary eligibility decision as the traditional reference assessment in 49 cases, giving an overall concordance of 98%. Agreement was high for both independent DAS runs (κ = 0.94; 95% CI 0.82–1.00 for each run), and the two DAS outputs were identical at the decision level. The exact McNemar test returned p = 1.00, which is consistent with the absence of a systematic direction to the single discordant call. In an exploratory TOST sensitivity analysis using a 5% non-inferiority margin, the exact 95% CI for concordance (0.894–0.999) crossed the prespecified 0.95 threshold, so formal non-inferiority was not demonstrated. This result should therefore be interpreted descriptively and not as evidence of statistical equivalence.

3.3.5 Error analysis
The observed discrepancies can be grouped into three error types: superfluous explanatory remarks that cited non-decisive features, omission of one non-decisive rationale element, and one misapplied eligibility criterion. The first two affected narrative exactness but not the binary eligibility call. The third produced the only true discordant decision. These findings are useful for prototype refinement because they identify where clearer trigger mapping, input validation, and human review may improve report quality. They do not provide evidence about safety in routine clinical use.
3.4 Performance summary and interpretation
Across the 50 synthetic cases, the prototype showed high decision-level concordance with the traditional guideline-based reference assessment, while exact narrative agreement was slightly lower. This distinction is important: the system more reliably reproduced the final eligible versus ineligible classification than the full wording of the accompanying explanation.
Taken together, the results support the feasibility of using the prototype to reproduce guideline-based eligibility decisions in controlled synthetic scenarios under human-supervised development conditions. They should not be interpreted as demonstrating expert-level clinical reasoning, patient benefit, or readiness for unsupervised deployment.
3.5 Application features and intended use
The web-based decision-aid system is intended to support the pre-test workflow for high-risk breast cancer under human oversight. After account creation, users receive introductory educational material and then enter personal information, clinical history, and a three-generation pedigree that can be drawn directly within the application or uploaded. The system organizes these inputs for guideline-based eligibility assessment, generates explanatory summaries, and provides follow-on educational modules about inheritance, multigene testing, possible result categories, and the benefits and limitations of testing. When family-history information is incomplete, the prototype is intended to document the available data, flag the case as insufficient information, and prompt clinician review rather than infer missing relatives or unverified cancer histories. A Chatbot is available throughout the workflow to answer common questions and reinforce information already grounded in the knowledge base. In its current form, the prototype is best understood as a counselling support and documentation tool rather than a replacement for clinician judgement.
A structured comparison with representative risk models, eligibility checkers, telegenetics workflows, and pedigree software is provided in Supporting Data 10. The intended contribution of GeneticBot is not a novel guideline rule set, but the integration of pedigree capture, guideline-grounded rationale generation, educational content, and documentation support within a single prototype workflow.

4. Discussion
This study describes the development of a web-based decision aid for pre-test genetic counselling in hereditary breast cancer and reports an early-stage synthetic-case evaluation of its eligibility workflow. The principal finding is that, within a controlled synthetic environment, the prototype showed high concordance with a traditional guideline-based reference assessment and perfect internal reproducibility of the binary eligibility label. These findings support feasibility of the prototype as a counselling-support artifact, but they do not constitute clinical validation.
The potential value of the prototype lies in workflow integration rather than in replacement of specialist care. The system combines structured intake, pedigree capture, guideline-aligned prompts, educational content, and printable summaries within a single interface. Such integration may help standardize documentation and prepare patients for counselling visits in settings where genetics expertise is limited. However, its intended use remains supportive and human supervised: final clinical interpretation, contextual judgement, and patient counselling still require appropriately trained professionals.
The performance metrics should be interpreted cautiously. The 98% decision-level concordance and κ of 0.94 were obtained from 50 fully synthetic cases using a reference assessment based on the same guideline framework. This creates a closed evaluation environment that is useful for feasibility testing and rule checking, but insufficient for claims about clinical effectiveness, generalizability, or expert-level reasoning. Similarly, perfect reproducibility across repeated runs reflects stability of the structured, rule-constrained workflow on identical inputs; it does not address variability in real-world family histories, data quality, or clinician-patient interaction.
Clarifying the boundary between deterministic logic and the language-model component is important. In the evaluated prototype, eligibility was anchored to explicit NCCN triggers derived from structured inputs, while the language model was used to organize explanatory summaries grounded in retrieved guideline passages. Even so, the observed narrative discrepancies highlight relevant failure modes, including superfluous remarks and imperfect rationale mapping. These findings support the need for safeguards such as explicit trigger display, restricted retrieval sources, input validation, audit trails, and human review before any real-world use.
The study should also be positioned within the broader landscape of digital genetics services. Existing approaches such as telegenetics, mainstreaming workflows, educational decision aids, and pedigree software have already shown that parts of the counselling pathway can be standardized or delivered remotely (6,7,17–19). Accordingly, we avoid broad first-of-its-kind claims. The present contribution is the integration of several of these functions into a single prototype for pre-test hereditary breast-cancer counselling. Its relevance to the MENA region is currently conceptual rather than empirically demonstrated, because the present evaluation used NCCN-based logic and did not test region-specific datasets, workflows, or patient outcomes.
Several limitations should be emphasized. First, the study used only synthetic cases and therefore cannot establish clinical validity, safety, or patient benefit. Second, the sample size was appropriate for feasibility work but not for formal equivalence testing. Third, the reference assessment and the prototype were both anchored to the same guideline framework, limiting claims about independent reasoning. Fourth, we did not evaluate user-centered outcomes such as understanding, decisional conflict, anxiety, usability, or acceptability, and the proposed questionnaires included in Supporting Data 7 were not administered in this phase. Future work should include prospective ethics-approved evaluation with real clinical data, predefined statistical objectives, structured error monitoring, and assessment of workflow, educational, and patient-centered outcomes.
In conclusion, GeneticBot should presently be understood as an early-stage decision-aid prototype rather than a clinically validated system. Its synthetic-case performance suggests that guideline-based eligibility assessment and documentation support can be operationalized in a web-based workflow, but substantial additional work is required before clinical deployment. That next phase should include prospective real-world validation, explicit governance and accountability processes, continued human oversight, and clear clinician responsibility for final decisions.
