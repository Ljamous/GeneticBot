Abstract:
Genetic counselling remains essential for hereditary-cancer care, yet access is constrained by workforce shortages, especially in low-resource settings. We developed 
GeneticBot
, a web-based decision aid that guides pre-test counselling and automates eligibility checks for hereditary breast-cancer testing aligned to established criteria
; 
National Comprehensive Cancer Network
 (NCCN
)
. The tool provides structured intake, risk prompts, documented rationales, and printable summaries for patients and clinicians. We evaluated 
GeneticBot
 using a set of 50 synthetic, non-identifiable test cases to assess concordance of eligibility decisions and the clarity of generated counselling summaries. The pilot demonstrated consistent, reproducible determinations and streamlined documentation, supporting feasibility of digital pre-test triage. 
GeneticBot
 is not a substitute for clinical judgement; rather, it augments counsellor workflows and can help prioritize limited appointments. Wider deployment could improve access, standardize decision-making, and reduce bottlenecks in regions where genetic-counselling capacity is limited.
Introduction:
Genetic counselling is central to identifying individuals at elevated risk of hereditary cancers, particularly breast cancer. 
The 
NCCN Clinical Practice Guidelines in Oncology (NCCN Guidelines
)
s for Genetic/Familial High-Risk Assessment: Breast, Ovarian and Pancreatic 
offer a widely adopted framework for risk stratification, testing eligibility and downstream management, and are used across cancer centres and research programs to promote consistent, evidence-based decisions. In practice, adherence is variable, most notably in settings with limited counselling capacity, because application of the guidelines requires detailed appraisal of family history, tumour features and personal risk factors, which is both expertise-intensive and time-consuming. Consequently, some high-risk patients remain untested or receive incomplete pre-test counselling. Implementing scalable, guideline-concordant workflows, incorporating structured documentation, patient education and accommodation for local constraints, could improve access and equity in hereditary cancer risk assessment
 
(1,2)
 .
 
Approximately 5–10% of breast cancers are attributable to inherited pathogenic variants, most commonly in the high-penetrance genes BRCA1 and BRCA2 
(3)
. In unselected, population-based cohorts, pathogenic variants across established predisposition genes are detected in ~5% of cases, consistent with large NEJM analyses (e.g., 5.03% across 12 genes) 
(4)
. This burden underpins guideline-driven recommendations for genetic risk assessment and testing. At the same time, many regions
, 
particularly in the Middle East
, 
face a marked shortage of trained genetic counsellors, 
often rely 
heavily on physician-led or ad-hoc models to meet rising demand and the growing complexity of genomic interpretation 
(5)
. Consequently, alternative service-delivery approaches (telephone/video 
telegenetics
, 
mainstreaming, group counselling) and digital tools (decision aids, intake chat assistants, algorithmic triage) have expanded to improve access, efficiency, and patient knowledge, with professional guidelines and systematic reviews supporting comparable outcomes to in-person counselling when implemented appropriately 
(6,7)
.
The 
introduction
 of massively parallel sequencing reduced time and cost enabled multigene panel testing, and improved diagnostic yield 
(8,9)
. 
However, challenges persist, including interpreting variants of uncertain significance
 (VUS)
 and managing large volumes of genetic data
 
(10,11)
. 
To address these challenges, 
NCCN
 
Guidelines
 
recommend
 genetic testing for hereditary cancers
 
(12)
. However, adherence to these 
Guidelines
 varies, especially in regions with limited healthcare infrastructure.
Across the Middle East, genomic medicine is increasingly prioritized, with Egypt, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Jordan, and the UAE advancing national initiatives, training programs, and service expansion 
(5,13)
. 
Large-scale population genomics programs and local training initiatives have expanded genetic services in these nations.
 
However, genetic counselling remains in its early stages in many parts of the region. The high prevalence of consanguinity and endogamy-related genetic disorders has 
driven demand for services
 
(14)
. 
Despite this need, there are only about 40 trained genetic counsellors serving over 450 million people, primarily based in Saudi Arabia and the UAE
 
(15)
. This shortage presents significant challenges, particularly in underserved areas, where access to qualified professionals remains scarce.
 
Barriers to genetic counselling in the Middle East 
include
 
l
imited
 availability of trained specialists 
(16)
, 
c
ultural and ethical concerns
 related to genetic testing and disclosure of hereditary risks, healthcare system limitations such as inadequate 
and lack of formal genetic counselling programs. Addressing these issues requires innovative service models, increased regional training programs, and greater integration of digital tools to support genetic counselling.
These challenges underscore the need for digital approaches that simplify pre-test education, bring consistency to risk assessment, and cut administrative load. Telemedicine, decision-support systems, and interactive tools can structure education, standardize evaluations, and lessen the workload for genetic counsellors
 
(17–19)
. 
To address this gap, our study developed 
GeneticBot
, 
a web-based decision aid that operationalizes the 
NCCN Guidelines
 
to automate pre-test eligibility assessment and provide structured, accessible pre-test genetic counselling. By embedding 
National Comprehensive Cancer Network
 (NCCN
)
 
criteria within a digital decision-support framework, the system aims to enhance accessibility, standardize triage, and support informed decision-making in regions where trained genetic counsellors are limited
.

Demonstration and Evaluation: 
The first increment phase assessed the literature review’s depth and relevance, using a pre-test genetic counselling questionnaire to identify gaps in service accessibility and efficiency. Likert-scale responses evaluated whether proposed solutions addressed care gaps for high-risk breast cancer patients, guiding refinements to align interventions with clinical needs. The second increment phase involved expert assessments of the framework’s information quality, media clarity, and structural logic. The third increment phase focused on testing the system’s effectiveness using synthetic cases to evaluate its accuracy in identifying high-risk patients according to 
NCCN Guidelines
. For future validation, additional evaluation tools were developed, including multiple-choice questions to assess patient understanding of the risks and benefits of genetic testing, and a comparative questionnaire to evaluate DAS versus the traditional approach (TA). Feedback from both healthcare providers and patients was also incorporated to improve system refinement and integration into clinical practice. To maintain guideline compliance, automated API updates, manual audits, version control, and NCCN collaboration were proposed to sustain accuracy and usability in clinical practice.

RAG Framework & Sample Siz
e
:
We implemented a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework to operationalize NCCN Guidelines® (v3.2024) for pre-test eligibility and 
counselling
. We indexed a structured NCCN knowledge base for semantic retrieval. For each case, the system extracted key clinical and pedigree fields, then retrieved the most relevant NCCN passages. It used these to construct a grounded prompt for GPT-4, which generated a rationale-rich eligibility report with explicit NCCN triggers. Knowledge maintenance relied on authorized guideline access
 and 
version control. To evaluate feasibility, spectrum coverage, and preliminary accuracy, we constructed 50 fully synthetic cases. Each case comprised a three-generation pedigree and a pathology report. We assumed 30–35 eligible (positive) cases. For this set, a 95% CI for sensitivity would be approximately ±12–15% if true sensitivity is 0.80–0.90, which we deemed acceptable for phase-1 validation 
(23)
. The case mix intentionally spanned the full range of DAS indication criteria, including borderline and rare scenarios. This approach was used to reduce spectrum bias and stress-test decision rules. Representativeness will be addressed in a subsequent consecutive or prevalence-weighted cohort. Two blinded non-genetics operators ran the system twice per case to assess reproducibility. A genetic 
counsellor
 adjudicated outputs against a Traditional Approach (TA) benchmark. Across the 50 cases, 
DAS achieved 98% decision-level concordance with 
TA (κ≈0.94), sensitivity 1.00, specificity ~0.91, and perfect decision-level reproducibility (κ=1.0), with narrative differences confined to non-critical notes.
 
Methods used for 
the 
validation study:
We profiled the cohort, computing ranges, means, and frequency distributions for age, receptor status, and pedigree complexity.  The same dataset was independently processed twice, in random order, by DAS operators who were blinded to TA criteria. For each run we cross-tabulated DAS output against TA label, yielding a 2 × 2 confusion matrix from which sensitivity, specificity, positive- and negative-predictive values, overall accuracy, and their exact 95% confidence intervals were calculated.  The chance-corrected agreement was summarised with Cohen’s κ, and systematic discordance between paired proportions was checked with the exact McNemar test (two-sided, α = 0.05).  To visualize discriminative ability, we plotted receiver-operating-characteristic (ROC) curves and estimated the area under the curve (AUC) with 95 % CIs.  Reproducibility was assessed by cross classifying the two independent DAS runs.  All analyses were performed in R 
4.4.1.
Results:
 
Identifying Shortcomings in The 
Pre-test genetic
 Counselling Phase
This study examined the transition from single-gene testing to multigene panel testing facilitated by Massively Parallel Sequencing (MPS) technology, with a focus on time and cost efficiency, the challenges in managing variants of uncertain significance (VUS), and the application of genomic sequencing across diverse patient phenotypes. Different genetic counselling models, including face-to-face, telephonic, videoconferencing, and group counselling, were analyzed to assess their effectiveness, accessibility, and scalability. The findings emphasized the need for innovative service delivery models that enhance patient access while maintaining the quality of genetic counselling services.
The outcome of this phase serves as a foundation for subsequent research and implementation steps, guiding the development of digital tools to improve accessibility and efficiency in genetic counselling. Expanding genetic services to underserved areas, where specialists are scarce, is a critical goal. This phase highlights the importance of integrating online decision-making tools and 
Chatbot
 technologies to support various stages of the genomic testing pathway, from pre-test education to post-test counselling. The effectiveness of these tools will be assessed based on their ability to identify eligible high-risk patients, alleviate patient anxiety, enhance genetic understanding, and simplify the counselling process. Studies have shown that, compared to traditional methods, 
telegenetics
 and video-based counselling improve patient engagement and comprehension. Moving forward, the focus will be on ensuring these digital solutions effectively address the identified gaps in pre-test genetic counselling and contribute to better patient outcomes and healthcare resource utilization.
A Structured Knowledge Base Framework
The framework comprises two core components: the DAS and a 
Chatbot
. The process is initiated with the DAS module, which begins with introductory materials, a script, and a video explaining genetic 
counselling
 and testing to orient users to the process. It then outlines the DAS system through a script and video summarizing the Genetic Testing Journey, guiding patients through key stages. The system requires users to input personal information, clinical history, and a three-generation family pedigree to evaluate eligibility against 
NCCN
 criteria. Educational modules cover genetic concepts (inheritance modes, multigene panels, and test outcomes) and 
test
 risks/benefits through paired scripts and videos for clarity. The framework adapts based on patient decisions (agreeing or declining testing), providing tailored next steps. The DAS aims to simplify complex genetic information, support informed consent, and align patient choices with clinical 
Guidelines
 by integrating multimedia resources, structured data collection, and decision pathways.
The 
Chatbot
 will serve as the second part of the framework, designed to support high-risk breast cancer patients by addressing frequently asked questions (FAQs) across multiple 
pre-test genetic
 
counselling
 domains, including personal and family history, psychological, educational, clinical, sociological, and physiological aspects. Additionally, it will provide information on high-penetrance breast cancer susceptibility genes such as BRCA1, BRCA2, CDH1, PALB2, PTEN, and TP53, along with references for commonly asked questions. Beyond the 
Chatbot
, the framework will incorporate multiple-choice questions to measure patient understanding of genetic testing, covering its benefits, risks, limitations, and uncertainties. Finally, a questionnaire has been developed to systematically assess the
 
DAS system in a genetic clinic setting, comparing its effectiveness to the
 
TA to ensure informed decision-making and optimize patient experience as 
a future plan
.

Reproducibility of DAS output
:
Internal reproducibility was perfect: the two independent DAS runs agreed on all 50 cases (40 TP, 10 TN, κ = 1.0). 
This shows that the model consistently distinguishes eligible from ineligible patients with high sensitivity and minimal false positives, confirming reproducible diagnostic performance
 (see Table 3).
Agreement with Traditional Expert Review
:
Across the 50-case validation set, the DAS reached the same eligibility criteria as the TA in 49 cases, giving an overall concordance of 98 %. Agreement was high for both independent DAS runs (AI-1 and AI-2): Cohen’s κ = 0.94 (95 % CI 0.82 – 1.00) against the manual benchmark in each run, and a perfect κ = 1.00 when the two AI outputs were compared with each other. An exact McNemar test of the TA-vs-AI disagreement cells returned p = 1.00, indicating no systematic bias in the rare discordant call. Taken together, these figures place DAS solidly in the “almost-perfect” agreement range, (see

Despite these errors, the system's binary eligible vs not eligible judgment remained unchanged, so clinical management was not affected

Performance Evaluation
 
S
ummary
 of the 
DAS
:
Across the 50 breast cancer cases, the DAS correctly flagged 46 as meeting 
NCCN
 genetic-testing criteria, yielding an accuracy of 92 %. 
The four discrepancies were limited to either the omission of a single criterion or the addition of an irrelevant observation; none changed clinical judgment in a way that would jeopardize the patient's management
. When the same data set was processed twice, the full narrative output from the two DAS iterations matched in 46 cases (92 %). In the remaining four, differences were restricted to explanatory sentences. At the same time, the underlying eligibility designation (“eligible” vs “not eligible”) remained identical in every case demonstrating high textual reproducibility and perfect decision-level consistency.
 Comparing DAS decisions with the traditional review produced an overall concordance of 98 % and Cohen’s κ of 0.94 for each run, indicating almost perfect agreement. Agreement between the two DAS runs on the binary eligibility flag was flawless (100 %, κ = 1.00). Both runs achieved sensitivity = 1.00, specificity ≈ 0.91, and identical AUCs of 0.96, underscoring robust diagnostic performance
.

Discussion 
This project developed an innovative genetic 
counselling
 support system combining a decision aid and 
Chatbot
 to improve accessibility and efficiency in 
pre-test genetic
 
counselling
 for high-risk breast cancer patients. Rooted in a thorough analysis of 
NCCN
 
Guidelines
 and existing literature, the framework addresses gaps in traditional methods by enhancing patient education, streamlining decision-making, improving communication, saving time for patients and healthcare providers, and reducing resources. 
The system integrates clinical and family history taking, educational modules, benefits and risks of genetic testing, consent, and real-time information/consultation
. By ensuring critical details are shared before consent and reducing reliance on in-person sessions, the tool aims to increase patient understanding and engagement, making genetic 
counselling
 more effective, accessible, and patient-
centered
.
 
One of the key innovative features of this application is its built-in pedigree drawing tool, which sets it apart from traditional methods. Typically, genetic 
counsellor
s manually construct pedigrees or rely on third-party software that requires an annual license, adding to 
the overall cost of genetic 
counselling
 services. Our
 
DAS
 
system integrates an automated pedigree drawing function, allowing users to create their family history diagram within the application. Users can either draw the pedigree while using the DAS application after watching a guided demonstration video or upload a hand-drawn pedigree, which the system then processes. In both cases, AI-driven tools extract relevant clinical and genetic information, integrating it with other patient
’s
 data. This AI-assisted approach automates risk classification by assessing eligibility for genetic testing based on the 
NCCN
 criteria, thereby streamlining decision-making and enhancing the efficiency of genetic 
counselling
 services.
Our validation of the DAS on 
50 constructed
 synthetic cases shows that the platform delivers expert-level performance while preserving clinical safety. DAS identified 
NCCN
-eligible patients with perfect sensitivity and 0.91 specificity, attaining 98 % overall agreement (κ = 0.94) with traditional manual review and an AUC of 0.96. Two independent runs produced identical eligibility calls (κ = 1.00), underscoring excellent internal reproducibility, and the few discrepancies were confined to non-critical explanatory notes that left the “
eligible
-versus-no
t-eligible
” decision unchanged.
 Despite these minimal variations, the DAS system effectively streamlined patient eligibility assessment, enhancing efficiency while maintaining accuracy.
 I
ntegrating Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) into the process could further improve the accuracy and consistency of the DAS system. HITL involves expert review and feedback, which helps correct errors, refine details, and ensure the AI properly interprets complex or nuanced data. This process enables the AI to learn and adapt over time, resulting in more reliable outputs. Additionally, HITL enhances precision by ensuring reports follow a consistent structure, use appropriate terminology, and remain aligned with evolving requirements. By combining AI efficiency with human oversight, this approach makes 
reports more accurate, structured, and trustworthy, reinforcing the potential of the DAS application as a reliable tool for clinical decision-making 
(24)
 . 
The use of a Decision Support Aid (DSA) and 
Chatbot
 for 
pre-test genetic
 
counselling
 based on 
NCCN
 
Guidelines
 in high-risk breast cancer patients presents both benefits and challenges, particularly in the MENA region. Biases may arise due to differences in genetic factors, cultural norms, healthcare systems, and resource availability compared to Western populations
. One notable bias emerges from the differences in genetic homogeneity. The breast cancer genes that define MENA populations and Western populations may not be the same, and such differences will be problematic if DSA and 
Chatbot
 are determined using data from the US alone. Another bias rests on cultural factors. Certain beliefs that are held concerning culture in the MENA area could affect the willingness of individuals to seek genetic counselling and testing services and could, therefore, render such services underutilized. Healthcare system disparities are also sources of bias. 
MENA populations have very limited access
 to genetic counselling and testing services compared to the western ones, which might interfere with the efficient use of such instruments. These issues have been featured in the literature regarding applying Western 
Guidelines
 in other literature and health systems settings 
(25)
.
To address these biases, one key strategy is to adapt the 
NCCN
 
Guidelines
 to align with the genetic, cultural, and healthcare context of the MENA region. This process involves collaboration with local experts and healthcare providers, as demonstrated in the 
NCCN
’s initiative to tailor oncology 
Guidelines
 for the MENA region. Committees of regional oncology specialists reviewed the original 
NCCN
 
Guidelines
 and worked with 
NCCN
 experts to adjust them based on local evidence and clinical practices. For breast and ovarian cancer genetic assessment, the adapted 
Guidelines
 for MENA remain more than 95% consistent with the original 
NCCN
 
Guidelines
, highlighting their applicability while 
addressing regional nuances. This effort has fostered greater evidence-based practice and collaboration within the region 
(26,27)
. Also, altering the DSA and 
Chatbot
 programs by including data on the region's genetics and epidemiology would make them more precise and relevant to the targeted audience. Furthermore, redesigning the 
Chatbot
 to be culturally appropriate and training it to understand the context of the MENA region can also increase the acceptance of the device among the people. This can be achieved by involving the stakeholders, such as the patients, health providers, and community leaders so that the interventions are relevant to them. Finally, the DSA and Chat Bot must continually be evaluated and updated per user expectations and information emerging from the area to remain effective. 
By incorporating the methods discussed above, these tools should address the gaps in pre-test genetic counselling services available in the MENA region and promote genetic 
equity 
(28)
.
The real-world validation involving both patients and healthcare providers is essential to evaluate accessibility, efficiency, and predefined metrics (
see 
Supporting
 Data 7
) in comparison to traditional 
approaches.
 User feedback will be vital in assessing clarity, relevance, and satisfaction. At the same time, performance metrics on accessibility and efficiency will measure the hybrid approach's effectiveness using the DAS system and 
Chatbot
. The validation will focus on two key aspects: identifying suitable patients for genetic testing and improving efficiency and accessibility in 
pre-test genetic
 
counselling
 for high-risk breast cancer patients 
using real (human) as well as synthetic cases
. Future developments will integrate user-driven improvements.
In conclusion, t
he specialty of genetic counselling has rapidly developed, with the adoption of new technologies including 
Chatbot
s and decision aid support to meet increasing demand. There have been significant advances in AI based 
Chatbot
s and decision support systems which have made it possible for a larger population to receive and utilize the pre-test genetic 
counselling services effectively. Other barriers of the genetic 
counselling
 have also included the insufficiency of enough genetic counsellor. In these regards, however, relatively good 
telegenetics
 and collaborative models’ advances have been done in more genetic testing practice operational comfort. These developments highlight the urgent need for adaptation and global alignment of the profession to ensure effective, timely, and individualized care. All practitioners in genetic counselling must work toward evolving practice standards to meet the demands of modern healthcare systems.
