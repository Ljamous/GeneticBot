Dear Authors,

Thank you for submitting your manuscript, “GeneticBot web decision aid for pre-test genetic counselling in high-risk breast cancer,” to PLOS Digital Health. The reviewers recognize the importance of your work and its potential contribution to addressing gaps in access to genetic counselling, particularly in resource-constrained settings.

However, after careful consideration, the decision is Major Revision. While the topic is timely and the proposed system is promising, substantial revisions are required to address critical concerns regarding study design, interpretation of findings, and alignment between evidence and claims.

The most important issues to address are outlined below:

1. Clarify the nature of the study and avoid overinterpretation of performance

A central concern across reviewers is that the current evaluation relies entirely on a small synthetic dataset and a guideline-based reference standard. This creates a closed-loop validation framework, where the system is effectively reproducing rule-based logic rather than demonstrating independent clinical reasoning or real-world performance.

Accordingly:

Reframe the study explicitly as feasibility or concordance testing, not clinical validation
Avoid terms such as “expert-level performance” or claims of clinical effectiveness
Clearly distinguish between rule replication and predictive or clinical performance

2. Address limitations of synthetic-only validation

The absence of real-world clinical data significantly limits interpretability and generalizability. While synthetic testing may be appropriate for early-stage development, the manuscript must:

Explicitly acknowledge this limitation
Clarify what conclusions can and cannot be drawn
Avoid implying clinical utility, safety, or deployment readiness

3. Strengthen statistical rigor and interpretation

Concerns were raised regarding the appropriateness of statistical methods and interpretation of performance metrics:

Reconsider the use of equivalence language without appropriate statistical testing frameworks
Provide clearer justification for analytical choices
Interpret performance metrics cautiously given the small sample size and deterministic structure of the system

4. Clarify system architecture and decision logic

The manuscript currently lacks sufficient clarity regarding:

The boundary between rule-based components and LLM-driven components
Whether eligibility decisions are deterministic or influenced by generative AI
How the system mitigates hallucination risk and ensures reliability

Please provide a clearer architectural description and explicitly address safety mechanisms.

5. Expand discussion of safety, ethics, and governance

Given the clinical context, the manuscript should more thoroughly address:

Potential failure modes and error types
Safeguards and validation layers
Clinical accountability and intended use
Regulatory and ethical considerations

6. Improve positioning within existing literature and tools

The manuscript would benefit from:

A structured comparison with existing decision aids and digital tools
Clear articulation of what is novel
Avoidance of unsupported “first-of-its-kind” claims

7. Address reporting and reproducibility issues

Please ensure:

Compliance with PLOS data availability requirements
Clarification of IRB status or exemption
Improved referencing and formatting consistency

8. Minor revisions

Please also address the specific suggestions provided by Reviewer 1, including clarification of human-in-the-loop components and handling of incomplete clinical data.

Given the scope of revisions required, this manuscript will require substantial restructuring to align claims with evidence and to clarify its contribution as an early-stage feasibility study. We encourage a careful and transparent revision, supported by a detailed point-by-point response to all reviewer comments.

Reviewer #1: Thank you for letting me review such an interesting topic and research. Please find the below comments for further improvements at any future follow-ups for your project:

Reviewer #2: 1. This Design Science Research (DSR) study tests GeneticBot (GPT-4 with RAG) against NCCN v3.2024 using 50 synthetic cases. The approach lacks a prespecified null hypothesis for equivalence testing. The authors commit to circular validation logic, testing an LLM on synthetic data. This risks "LLM evaluating LLM" and changes study implications to functionality testing.
2. Perfect sensitivity (100%) and 98% specificity (κ=0.94) were achieved, but with only 50 synthetic cases (39 positive). The McNemar test (p=1.00) cannot establish equivalence; Two One-Sided Tests (TOST) with predefined margins (e.g., ±5%) are required per Altman-Bland.
3. While addressing MENA's genetic counselor shortage (~40 for 450M people) is important, synthetic-only validation provides no empirical evidence of real-world clinical utility or patient safety.
4. Consider the following to improve the submission:
4.1. Provide IRB exemption letter with date and protocol number; only IRB determines whether study could be exempted; the authors do conduct the study on medical professionals (https://www.hhs.gov/ohrp/regulations-and-policy/guidance/index.html);
4.2. Conduct TOST with 5% non-inferiority margin (https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.311.7003.485);
4.3 Generate and test on additional synthetic cases (n≥400 per Kodenko et al., https://doi.org/10.1134/S1064562424602063IF: 0.6 Q3 ) as incremental cost is marginal;
4.4. Deposit code/data for study replication in GitHub with DOI via Zenodo integration.
5. Claims of "expert-level performance" and "improved access" overreach the evidence; synthetic concordance does not demonstrate clinical effectiveness.
6. Tabular data is informative; 18 megabytes of supplementary PDFs is excessive and only marginally contribute to study replicability.
7. Large text volume and low-quality referencing with duplicates (References 2 and 15), Vancouver violations (References 2, 13, 25) decrease the overall impression. No IRB exemption letter is provided. Incomplete adherence to STARD-AI (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40954311/).

Addressing the points mentioned above would require substantial revision with new data collection.

Reviewer #3: Overinterpretation of diagnostic performance

The reported metrics are strong:

Sensitivity = 1.00
Specificity ≈ 0.91
Accuracy = 0.98
κ = 0.94

However, these are derived from:

A small sample size (n = 50)
A synthetic dataset
A benchmark (TA) based on the same guideline logic

This creates a closed-loop validation problem, where:

The model is essentially reproducing rule-based logic it was built upon
True generalization is not tested

Clarify that this is concordance with rule-based expert interpretation, not predictive performance
Avoid framing results as “expert-level performance”
Emphasize that this is decision-rule replication, not independent clinical reasoning

ack of comparison with existing tools or workflows

The manuscript does not sufficiently position GeneticBot relative to:

Existing digital decision aids
Telegenetics platforms
Commercial pedigree tools
Other AI-based triage systems

Although the pedigree drawing feature is highlighted as novel, the claim that this is “first” is not adequately substantiated.
Provide a structured comparison with prior tools
Clarify what is genuinely novel (integration? automation? RAG? workflow?)
Avoid unsupported “first-of-its-kind” claims

Ambiguity in AI vs rule-based components

The system is described as:

AI-driven (GPT-4, RAG framework)
NCCN rule-based decision engine

However, the boundary between:

Deterministic rule application
Generative AI reasoning

is not clearly defined.

This is important

Clinical reliability depends on deterministic components
LLMs introduce variability and hallucination risk


Which components are rule-based vs LLM-based
Whether eligibility decisions are deterministic
Whether the LLM can influence classification outcomes

Risk of hallucination and safety not sufficiently addressed

Although the manuscript mentions minor narrative inconsistencies, it does not deeply address:

Hallucination risks from LLM integration
Failure modes in edge cases
Safety mechanisms (guardrails, validation layers)

For example, errors such as incorrect NCCN triggers were observed.
Provide a structured error taxonomy
Describe safeguards preventing unsafe outputs
Clarify how incorrect reasoning is detected and corrected

Limited evaluation of user-centered outcomes

The manuscript focuses heavily on:

Diagnostic concordance
Technical performance

But does not evaluate:

Patient understanding
Decision quality
Anxiety reduction
Usability

Although questionnaires and educational modules are proposed, these are not empirically tested.

Given that this is a decision aid, evaluation should extend beyond accuracy to human outcomes.

Statistical interpretation needs caution
Confidence intervals are wide due to small sample size
AUC interpretation is limited given binary rule-based output
Perfect reproducibility (κ = 1.0) reflects deterministic design rather than robustness


The distinction between 92% narrative accuracy vs 98% decision accuracy is potentially confusing and should be clarified more transparently.

Generalizability concerns (MENA vs global applicability)

The manuscript emphasizes MENA applicability, but:

Uses NCCN (US-based) guidelines
